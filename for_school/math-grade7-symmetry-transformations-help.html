Symmetry & Transformations

Grade 7  ·  Lines of symmetry, reflection, rotation, translation

Practice Worksheet →
Types of Symmetry
Line Symmetry
Reflection symmetry

A shape has line symmetry if a line divides it into two mirror-image halves. Also called bilateral symmetry.

Rotational Symmetry
Order of rotation

A shape has rotational symmetry if it looks the same after rotating less than 360°. The order = number of positions in one full turn.

Point Symmetry
180° rotational

Special case of rotational symmetry of order 2. Rotating 180° about the centre gives the same shape.

The Four Transformations
Translation
Slide / shift

Every point moves the same distance in the same direction. Shape and size unchanged. Described by a vector (x, y).

Reflection
Mirror flip

Every point flips across a line of reflection. Shape and size unchanged but orientation reverses. Described by the mirror line.

Rotation
Turn about a point

Every point turns by an angle around a fixed centre. Shape and size unchanged. Described by centre, angle, and direction (CW/CCW).

Enlargement
Scale up/down

Every point moves away from (or towards) a centre by the same scale factor. Shape preserved but size changes. Described by centre and scale factor.

Worked Examples
1. How many lines of symmetry does a regular hexagon have?
A regular hexagon has 6 equal sides and 6 equal angles.
Each axis of symmetry passes through opposite vertices OR midpoints of opposite sides.
6 lines of symmetry
2. Reflect point A(3, 2) in the y-axis.
Reflecting in the y-axis: x → −x, y stays the same.
(3, 2) → (−3, 2)
A' = (−3, 2)
3. Translate point B(1, 4) by the vector (3, −2).
Add the vector to each coordinate:
New x = 1 + 3 = 4, New y = 4 + (−2) = 2
B' = (4, 2)
4. What is the order of rotational symmetry of a square?
A square looks identical at 90°, 180°, 270°, and 360° (back to start).
That is 4 positions in one full turn.
Order 4
5. A shape is enlarged by scale factor 3 from centre (0,0). Point C is at (2, 1). Find C'.
Multiply each coordinate by the scale factor.
C' = (2 × 3, 1 × 3)
C' = (6, 3)
Translation, reflection, and rotation are called isometries — they preserve size and shape. Enlargement changes size but preserves shape — it produces a similar figure, not necessarily a congruent one.
