Factors & Multiples

Grade 4 Math  ·  Understand divisibility, primes, and skip counting

🔢 Factors vs Multiples

Factor

A number that divides another number evenly (no remainder). Factors are always smaller than or equal to the number.

Factors of 12 → 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12

Multiple

A number you get by multiplying a number by 1, 2, 3, 4, … Multiples are always greater than or equal to the number.

Multiples of 4 → 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, …

Memory trick: Think of factors as what you can factor out of a number, and multiples as what appears in that number's times table.

🔍 Finding Factor Pairs

List factor pairs by starting at 1 and working up. A factor pair is two numbers that multiply to give your target.

Factor pairs of 12:

1 × 12
2 × 6
3 × 4

So all factors of 12 are: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12

Tip: Stop when the two numbers in a pair would cross over (e.g., after 3 × 4 we don't need 4 × 3 — that's the same pair).

🔁 Finding Multiples

Multiples of a number are just the results of its times table. Keep multiplying by 1, 2, 3, …

First 5 multiples of 7:

7 × 1 = 7
7 × 2 = 14
7 × 3 = 21
7 × 4 = 28
7 × 5 = 35

So the first 5 multiples of 7 are: 7, 14, 21, 28, 35

⭐ Prime, Composite, and Neither

Every whole number greater than 1 is either prime or composite:

Prime

Exactly 2 factors: 1 and itself. Cannot be divided evenly by anything else.

2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19…

Composite

More than 2 factors. Can be divided evenly by numbers other than 1 and itself.

4, 6, 8, 9, 10, 12, 14, 15…

Neither

The number 1 has only one factor (itself), so it is neither prime nor composite.

Just: 1

Note: 2 is the only even prime number. All other even numbers are composite because they are divisible by 2.

⚠️ Watch Out!

  • 1 IS NOT PRIME The number 1 has only one factor (itself). Prime numbers need exactly two factors. So 1 is neither prime nor composite.
  • 2 IS PRIME 2 is the only even prime. Every other even number has 2 as a factor, giving it more than 2 factors.
  • FACTOR ≠ MULTIPLE Factors are smaller (or equal), multiples are larger (or equal). Don't swap the words!
  • MISSING PAIRS When listing factors, work in pairs and don't skip any. Start from 1 and work up until the pair would cross.
