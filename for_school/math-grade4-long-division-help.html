Long Division

Long division lets you divide large numbers by breaking the problem into four repeating steps: Divide → Multiply → Subtract → Bring Down. Remember D-M-S-B and you'll never be lost.

⏱ ~6 min read 📝 Jump to Worksheet →

Section 1

What is Long Division?

Long division is a written method for dividing larger numbers. It works the same way as short division but shows every step clearly.

Key terms: the number being divided is the dividend, the number you divide by is the divisor, the answer is the quotient, and any leftover amount is the remainder.

Example: 85 ÷ 4 = 21 remainder 1 → dividend 85, divisor 4, quotient 21, remainder 1.

Section 2

The D-M-S-B Method

D
Divide
How many times does the divisor go into the current digits?
M
Multiply
Multiply the quotient digit by the divisor and write below.
S
Subtract
Subtract to find what's left.
B
Bring Down
Bring the next digit of the dividend down alongside the remainder.

Repeat D-M-S-B until there are no more digits to bring down. Whatever is left after the last subtraction is the remainder. A remainder must always be less than the divisor.

Section 3

Worked Examples

Example 1 — 96 ÷ 4 (no remainder)
Work out 96 ÷ 4.
D: 9 ÷ 4 = 2 (write 2 above) 4 goes into 9 twice
M: 2 × 4 = 8 (write below 9)
S: 9 − 8 = 1 (remainder so far)
B: Bring down 6 → now have 16
D: 16 ÷ 4 = 4 (write 4 above) 4 goes into 16 exactly four times
M: 4 × 4 = 16   S: 16 − 16 = 0
✅ Answer: 24
Example 2 — 78 ÷ 3 (no remainder)
Work out 78 ÷ 3.
D: 7 ÷ 3 = 2   M: 2×3=6   S: 7−6=1   B: bring 8 → 18
D: 18 ÷ 3 = 6   M: 6×3=18   S: 18−18=0
✅ Answer: 26
Example 3 — 85 ÷ 4 (with remainder)
Work out 85 ÷ 4.
D: 8 ÷ 4 = 2   M: 2×4=8   S: 8−8=0   B: bring 5 → 5
D: 5 ÷ 4 = 1   M: 1×4=4   S: 5−4=1 remainder 1
✅ Answer: 21 remainder 1
Example 4 — 126 ÷ 6 (3-digit dividend)
Work out 126 ÷ 6.
D: 1 ÷ 6 = 0 6 doesn't go into 1, so take two digits: 12
D: 12 ÷ 6 = 2   M: 2×6=12   S: 12−12=0   B: bring 6 → 6
D: 6 ÷ 6 = 1   M: 1×6=6   S: 6−6=0
✅ Answer: 21
Example 5 — 143 ÷ 7 (with remainder)
Work out 143 ÷ 7.
D: 14 ÷ 7 = 2   M: 2×7=14   S: 14−14=0   B: bring 3 → 3
D: 3 ÷ 7 = 0 7 doesn't go into 3; write 0 in quotient
M: 0×7=0   S: 3−0=3   No more digits → remainder 3
✅ Answer: 20 remainder 3

Section 4

Watch Out! ⚠️

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ❌ Remainder ≥ divisor: Finishing with a remainder of 5 when the divisor is 4.
    ✅ If remainder ≥ divisor, your quotient digit is too small — increase it by 1 and redo.
  • ❌ Skipping a 0 in the quotient: When the divisor doesn't go into the current digits, forgetting to write 0 and just moving on.
    ✅ Always write 0 in the quotient when the divisor is too big; then bring down the next digit.
  • ❌ Not bringing down the next digit: Stopping after the subtraction without bringing down.
    ✅ After every subtraction, immediately bring down the next digit before doing the next D step.

Section 5

Quick Reference

D-M-S-B
Divide → Multiply → Subtract → Bring Down. Repeat until done.
Remainder rule
Remainder must always be less than the divisor.
Check
(Quotient × Divisor) + Remainder = Dividend. e.g. 21×4+1 = 85 ✓
Two-digit start
If the divisor is bigger than the first digit, use the first two digits together.
Quotient 0
Write 0 in the quotient any time the divisor doesn't go into the current number.
Exact division
If the final remainder is 0, the division is exact with no remainder.
