Multiplication Tables

In this guide, you'll learn how to read, understand, and recall multiplication tables from 2 to 10 — and how to use them to solve problems fast.

⏱ ~5 min read 📝 Jump to Worksheet →

Section 1

What are Multiplication Tables?

Multiplication is a fast way to add the same number many times. Instead of writing 5 + 5 + 5 + 5, we can just say 4 × 5 = 20.

A multiplication table (also called a times table) lists all the products for one number multiplied by 1, 2, 3 … 10. Learning these by heart means you can solve problems instantly — no counting needed!

The two numbers being multiplied are called factors, and the result is called the product.

Section 2

Step-by-Step Method

Factor × Factor = Product
e.g.   6 × 7 = 42
  1. 1
    Read the problem. Find the two numbers (factors) being multiplied. E.g. "What is 7 × 8?"
  2. 2
    Find the row. In the times table grid, find the row for the first factor (7). Alternatively, choose the smaller number's table.
  3. 3
    Find the column. Move along that row until you reach the column for the second factor (8).
  4. 4
    Read the product. The number where the row and column meet is your answer. 7 × 8 = 56.
  5. 5
    Check with the flip. Multiplication works both ways — if 7 × 8 = 56, then 8 × 7 = 56 too. This is called the commutative property.
a × b = b × a
The order of factors doesn't change the product

Section 3

Worked Examples

Example 1 — Easy: Table of 2
What is 2 × 6?
2 × 6 means "2 groups of 6"
= 6 + 6 adding two sixes together
= 12
✅ Answer: 12
Example 2 — Easy: Table of 5
What is 5 × 7?
5 × 7 means "5 groups of 7"
Tip: multiples of 5 always end in 0 or 5
5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35 ← count 7 steps
= 35
✅ Answer: 35
Example 3 — Medium: Table of 9
What is 9 × 8?
9 × 8 use the finger trick or commutative property
= 8 × 9 same answer either way
Tip: 9 × n = (10 × n) − n
= (10 × 8) − 8 = 80 − 8 = 72
✅ Answer: 72
Example 4 — Hard: Missing Factor (Reverse Question)
? × 8 = 56   What is the missing number?
We need to find which number × 8 gives 56
Think: 8 × 1=8, 8×2=16, 8×3=24, 8×4=32,
       8×5=40, 8×6=48, 8×7=56 ✓
So ? = 7
✅ Answer: 7
Example 5 — Word Problem: Equal Groups
There are 6 bags. Each bag has 9 apples. How many apples are there in total?
Equal groups → use multiplication
Number of groups × size of each group
= 6 × 9
= 54
✅ Answer: 54 apples

Section 4

Watch Out! ⚠️

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ❌ Confusing multiplication with addition: Writing 4 × 3 = 7 (adding instead of multiplying)
    ✅ Remember: 4 × 3 means 4 groups of 3 = 3 + 3 + 3 + 3 = 12
  • ❌ Skipping a step in skip counting: For 5 × 6, counting "5, 10, 15, 25, 30" (jumped 20!)
    ✅ Count carefully: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30 — that's 6 steps = 30
  • ❌ Thinking order matters: Believing 3 × 7 is different from 7 × 3
    ✅ Multiplication is commutative: 3 × 7 = 7 × 3 = 21. Use whichever order you know better!

Section 5

Quick Reference

Key Formula
Factor × Factor = Product
Multiplication
Repeated addition of equal groups.
Factor
Each number being multiplied (e.g. in 4×3, both 4 and 3 are factors).
Product
The result of multiplying two factors (e.g. 4×3=12; 12 is the product).
Commutative Property
a × b = b × a. Order of factors doesn't matter.
Identity Property
Any number × 1 = itself. e.g. 7 × 1 = 7.
Zero Property
Any number × 0 = 0. e.g. 9 × 0 = 0.
