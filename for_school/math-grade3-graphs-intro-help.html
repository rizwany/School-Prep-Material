Pictographs & Bar Graphs

In this guide, you'll learn how to read pictographs and bar graphs, extract information from them, compare values, and calculate totals and differences.

⏱ ~5 min read 📝 Jump to Worksheet →

Section 1

What are Pictographs & Bar Graphs?

A pictograph uses pictures or symbols to represent data. Each symbol stands for a certain number (the key tells you how many). They're great for comparing categories at a glance.

A bar graph uses rectangular bars of different heights (or lengths) to show data. The taller the bar, the larger the value. The vertical axis (y-axis) shows the scale and numbers.

Both types of graphs have a title, labels for categories, and a scale or key to read the values.

Section 2

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Read the title. Understand what the graph is about — what data is being shown.
  2. 2
    Check the key or scale. For a pictograph, find what each symbol is worth. For a bar graph, read the numbers on the vertical axis to find the scale.
  3. 3
    Read the category labels. These are along the bottom (or side) of the graph — they tell you what each bar or row represents.
  4. 4
    Find the value. Count symbols (× key) for pictographs. Read the bar's height against the scale for bar graphs.
  5. 5
    Compare or calculate. To find the difference between two values, subtract. To find the total, add them up.
Pictograph value = symbols × key    Difference = larger − smaller

Section 3

Worked Examples

Use the Favourite Fruits pictograph above (key: each symbol = 2 students) for Examples 1–3.

Example 1 — Reading a Pictograph Value
How many students chose Mango as their favourite fruit?
Count mango symbols: 5 symbols
Each symbol = 2 students (key)
5 × 2 = 10 students
✅ Answer: 10 students
Example 2 — Comparing Values (Pictograph)
How many more students chose Apple than Orange?
Apple: 4 symbols × 2 = 8 students
Orange: 2 symbols × 2 = 4 students
Difference: 8 − 4 = 4
✅ Answer: 4 more students chose Apple
Example 3 — Total (Pictograph)
How many students are in the class altogether?
Apple=8, Banana=6, Mango=10, Orange=4
Total = 8+6+10+4 = 28 students
✅ Answer: 28 students
Example 4 — Reading a Bar Graph
Using the "Books Read Per Month" bar graph above — in which month were the most books read, and how many?
Look for the tallest bar → March (green bar)
Its height reaches 12 on the scale
✅ Answer: March — 12 books
Example 5 — Difference (Bar Graph)
How many more books were read in March than in February?
March = 12 books
February = 5 books
Difference = 12 − 5 = 7
✅ Answer: 7 more books in March

Section 4

Watch Out! ⚠️

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ❌ Ignoring the key in a pictograph: Saying 4 apples when 4 symbols × key of 2 = 8 students
    ✅ Always check the key first! Multiply the number of symbols by the key value.
  • ❌ Misreading the bar graph scale: Reading a bar between 4 and 6 as 4 or 6 instead of 5
    ✅ Look carefully at where the bar top lines up on the scale — it may land between two numbers.
  • ❌ Adding instead of subtracting for "how many more": Calculating difference as a sum
    ✅ "How many more" and "how many fewer" always require subtraction: bigger − smaller.

Section 5

Quick Reference

Pictograph
Graph using symbols. Value = count × key.
Bar Graph
Graph using bars. Value = bar height on the scale.
Key / Legend
Tells you what one symbol is worth in a pictograph.
Scale (y-axis)
The numbers on the vertical axis of a bar graph.
Total
Add all values together.
Difference
Subtract: larger value − smaller value.
